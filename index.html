About

Hi. My name is Joe Mattson, and I am a Mechanical Engineer studying at Berkeley. I prioritize learning highly, and as such, I have spent much of my time at Berkeley studying theories and learning fields. While studying, I quickly realized that some knowledge can only be gained through experience, and since have sought out fields that I am passionate about and excel in. Throughout my time at Berkeley, I have focused my learning and hands-on experience mainly on thermodynamics, fluid dynamics, heat transfer, neural networks, computer design, and manufacturing.

I have accrued experience in the aforementioned subjects through numerous projects, such as modeling Formula Electricâ€™s accumulator and motor cooling. Each project touched on a different aspect of cooling, with the accumulator project requiring knowledge of battery pack heat loads and a primary focus on air cooling. In contrast, motor cooling focused more on heat transfer and fluid dynamics, as FEB uses liquid cooling with a radiator for the motor and motor controller cooling loop. Other projects, such as Wildfire Prevention Research, have catapulted me into the world of AI, where I have learned about and contributed to transformers, encoders, and different architectures such as Convolutional Neural Networks and Retrieval Augmented Generation.

I have used my many projects and experiences to build a repertoire of knowledge gained through hands-on, real-world experiences. Dedicating yourself to learning theories behind ideas is strictly necessary; however, in my experience, theoretical knowledge can only be wholly understood after experiencing and discovering concepts through trial and error in real-world applications. Additionally, I have found that a vast array of knowledge is best gained through projects and experiences; furthermore, I have found that a large sum of knowledge can only be achieved through the trial and error projects bring, especially knowledge that aids in the efficiency and productivity of projects.

Formula Electric at Berkeley

As am a member of the Simulations subteam on Formula Electric Berkeley. I lead a smaller simulations subteam: Sims Thermals, under our main sims lead. Previously, I worked as a simulations engineer focused on vehicle dynamics, acting as an inbetween for the Dynamics subteam and Sims.

All of my FEB projects past and present are under the MATLAB and Solidworks tabs.

Vehicle Dynamics Simulation

This is my first project done on sims, utilizing MATLAB Simscape Multibody. - This project was aimed at generating camber curves. The simulation shown generated data exported to a csv, mapping out the camber curves of the tire in space, which allowed our Dynamics subteam to adjust their suspension geometry accordingly.

As a member of sims, I have done extensive work with both lap models and thermal simulations, as well as the initial project in vehicle dynamics. Additionally, while I have many iterations of our thermal coolant loops for our motor/motor controller and accumulator, I am unable to show these in such a public domain.

Nuclear Propulsion In Space

After making it through the application process, myself and five other undergraduate researchers spent the entire Fall2023 semester creating this policy paper from scratch. Many weeks were spent on the initial research and many weeks were spent coordinating the draft with our supervisor. I was given the task of researching Nuclear Propulsion Technology and its interworkings - a task best suited for myself with my engineering background. I also played a significant part in the proposal itself, coming up with many of the main talking points - even with no previous policy experience.

CAD Supervisor

NewSpace@Berkeley partnered with USRA to enable a student led project focused on creating a marketplace for satellite and advanced air mobility data. Having developed their use case, the students needed aid in bringing their solution to life through CAD. Pages 18 and 19 show their progress and my final result.

Serving as the CAD Supervisor, I stepped in and used my access to SOLIDWORKS to revamp the project and create a model that was additionally able to be 3D printed as a prototype for use in their presentation, which was later presented at NASA Ames. Feel free to check out the presentation.
